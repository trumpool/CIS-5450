§ 04 · Modeling

Predicting delay ≥ 15 min — before takeoff.

  • Target. DepDel15 — binary, delayed at least 15 min.
  • Split. Temporal — never random — prevents rolling-feature leakage.
  • Features. 23 engineered → 74 columns after one-hot, 8 categories.
  • Anti-leakage. All rolling features use shift(1) — only past feeds today.
  • Models. SGD-Logit → Random Forest → XGBoost / LightGBM (best params: n_estimators=400 · max_depth=6 · lr=0.05).
  • Tuning. RandomizedSearchCV — 30 configs · 3-fold CV · 500k subsample (Δ AUC vs full 5.6M < 0.002).

§ 04 · Imbalance Handling

SMOTE oversampling vs class weights — recall wins.

SMOTE inflates precision, but at a steep recall cost.

  • Weighted XGB — scale_pos_weight = 3.76. P 0.609 · R 0.553 · F1 0.580.
  • SMOTE XGB — oversample 500k → 789,958. P 0.809 · R 0.415 · F1 0.549.
  • SMOTE-style synthetic minority points can't fabricate realistic operational context — model becomes overconfident on its training-set neighbors.
  • Class-weight wins on recall and on F1 at much lower train cost (4 s vs 8 s).
F1 delta
+0.037weighted over SMOTE
University of PennsylvaniaCIS 5450 · Spring 202615 / 23
Slide 15 · SMOTE comparison · 0:35

"Two ways to handle 80:20 imbalance — re-weight the loss function with scale-pos-weight, or oversample the minority class with SMOTE."

"SMOTE pushes precision up to 0.81 — when it predicts delay, it's right four times out of five. But recall collapses to 0.42 — it only catches 42 percent of actual delays. Class weighting catches 55 percent at precision 0.61. Net F1 is 4 points higher for class weighting."

"The reason is that SMOTE's synthetic minority points can't capture operational context — flights aren't k-nearest-neighbor interpolations. Class weighting just shifts the loss balance without inventing data, which is the more conservative move."

§ 04 · Why Classification

We also tried predicting delay minutes.

Verdict. LightGBM beats linear by ~2× on R2 but typical error is still ±13.6 min — same order as the 15-min "delayed" threshold we care about. Classification is more practically useful for this problem. Ridge with α = 1.0 was indistinguishable from OLS — features are not strongly collinear. Train on classification-side features (no ArrDelay leakage); test set: 1.13M Nov–Dec flights.

University of PennsylvaniaCIS 5450 · Spring 202620 / 23
Slide 12 · Regression detour · 0:35

"Before we land on the insurance application, one quick detour. Classification is a coarse target — it just asks 'will the flight be delayed.' We also tested four regression models that predict the actual number of minutes."

"LightGBM regressor is the clear winner — RMSE 32.4 min, MAE 13.6 min, R2 0.27 — about double the explanatory power of a linear baseline. Linear and Ridge collapse to the same number, which tells us our features aren't strongly collinear."

"But here's the punchline: even the best regressor's typical error is plus or minus 13.6 minutes — the same order of magnitude as the 15-minute threshold that defines a delay in the first place. Plus the residuals are heteroskedastic — variance grows with the prediction. So for any operational decision the user actually cares about, classification gives more useful information than regression. That's why we pivoted to the binary target as our primary deliverable."
